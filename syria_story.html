December 8, 2024

A problem the world agreed to contain

On December 8, 2024, the government of Bashar al-Assad collapsed. Checkpoints disappeared. Prisons opened. Millions of Syrians loaded their cars and started driving home. But in Syria’s northeastern desert, one population remained frozen in place — warehoused in a sprawling detention camp called al-Hol.

For seven years, al-Hol had held tens of thousands of people, many with connections to ISIS. The population was warehoused indefinitely while the world debated what to do. The camp, home to over 75,000 at its peak, was a problem the international community agreed to contain rather than solve.

Coverage of Syria’s post-Assad future toggled between cautious optimism and familiar warnings about ISIS. What was largely missing was the story of what would happen when the infrastructure built to prevent that resurgence fell apart. In January 2026, it did.

Syria, 2025

8,325 communities. A fractured landscape.

Each dot marks a settlement monitored by humanitarian teams throughout 2025 — covering all 14 governorates, from the Mediterranean coast to the Iraqi border. Together they trace a country that remains, a year after the government’s fall, profoundly unsettled.

When al-Hol emptied in January 2026, U.S. intelligence estimated that 15,000 to 20,000 people — including radicalized foreign nationals from more than 40 countries — vanished into exactly this landscape. The exodus wasn’t orderly repatriation. It was chaos: systematic smuggling, mass escapes, and a handover between Kurdish and Syrian government forces that left tens of thousands unaccounted for.

The dots you see are not simply geography. They are where a country’s displacement crisis has settled — and where a new, uncharted chapter is beginning to unfold.

The Weight of Years

Six million people. And a widening gap.

5.96M

Even a year after the government fell, nearly 6 million Syrians were living somewhere other than their area of origin — not abroad, not in a camp in Turkey or Lebanon, but displaced inside Syria’s own borders. The blue across this map is the accumulated weight of that displacement.

In 2025, the historical correlation between displacement and aid fundamentally broke down for the first time. US aid to Syria dropped 58% — from $1.2 billion to $506 million — while the IDP population decreased only 12%. Aid declined far faster than the crisis did.

For Syrian civil society organizations now bearing the weight of reintegration, the gap between what is needed and what is available grows wider every day.

Rural Damascus

“Host communities refuse to receive them”

The ring of towns stretching south and east of Damascus — Jaramana, Ashrafiet Sahnaya, At-Tall, Jdidet Artuz — became Syria’s largest inland refuge. But as people now stream out of the country’s detention camps, many of these same communities are refusing to let them in.

“Host communities refuse to receive or allow entry to families leaving the al-Hol camp into Syrian neighborhoods or cities,” said Sheikmous Ahmed, head of the Office of Displaced and Refugee Affairs under the Kurdish-led administration. “There was concern that these individuals possessed extremist thought and ideology during their years among the organization or in al-Hol, and that this extremist thought might pose a danger to the communities they returned to.”

Jaramana alone holds 501,000 IDPs as of December 2025 — a figure larger than the population of Oslo. The nearly 2 million displaced in this governorate arrived over years of war. The question of who belongs, and who can return where, was never simple here. Now it is sharper than ever.

Aleppo

The city where thousands arrived unseen

Aleppo was already one of Syria’s most complex displacement hubs — its eastern neighborhoods shattered by siege, its Kurdish district absorbing hundreds of thousands of IDPs. Then, in January 2026, something else arrived: thousands of people from al-Hol, moved into the city under circumstances that humanitarian organizations never witnessed.

“When the factions entered the camp, thousands of people were smuggled into Aleppo,” said Sheikmous Ahmed, “under the eyes of the vehicles of the factions that entered the camp, away from the eyes of the international community and humanitarian organizations.”

By December 2025, Aleppo held 1.28 million IDPs and had received 506,000 returnees — more than any other governorate. It is simultaneously Syria’s greatest return success and one of its most complex displacement crises. What it absorbed from al-Hol was added to that — silently, and without a count.

Southern Idleb

Return does not mean recovery

Maarrat An-Numan was repeatedly bombed, its market flattened, its residents evacuated north in waves. By 2020, the town was emptied. In December 2024, it changed hands. By December 2025, 36,000 people had returned — the highest single-location returnee count anywhere in Syria.

Many came back to discover their homes occupied by strangers, or collapsed entirely, or stripped bare. They returned anyway. But around them, communities are still deciding whom to accept — and the criteria are not always clear.

“Some were simply caught in the wrong place at the wrong time,” said Abu Qaseem, an administrator at the Stabilization Support Unit, an NGO running repatriation programs across Syria. “And then there were also children and trafficking victims.” For communities still scarred by years of conflict, the distinction may matter less than the association. On the ground, it is difficult for local communities to accept camp detainees with open arms, regardless of their actual connection to ISIS.

Idleb governorate received 504,000 returnees in 2025 — second-highest in the country. The urgency to come home runs directly against the ambiguity of what home has become.

Hama

“We’re doing everything we can”

Hama’s river valleys and farmland were contested for years. Towns like Madiq Castle, Halfaya, and Kafr Zeita sat on shifting front lines — shelled, abandoned, fought over, emptied. By late 2024, most of Hama passed out of the previous government’s control. By December 2025, 344,000 people had returned — the third-highest returnee total in the country.

Into this fragile return movement stepped small Syrian organizations trying to manage what the international community had declined to plan for. The Stabilization Support Unit (SSU) has been running what it calls “Hope Convoys” — organized returns of Syrian families to their home provinces, including Homs, Daraa, Idleb, Raqqa, and Deir ez-Zor.

“Camps in Aleppo and Erbil where many al-Hol detainees have been transferred were not built to handle such an influx, but we’re doing everything we can,” said Abu Qaseem. “We’re very grateful for the local communities who have been donating essential supplies and resources.”

The scale of what followed al-Hol’s collapse dwarfed anything they had prepared for.

Crossing Back

583,000 returned. Some couldn’t.

583K

More than half a million Syrians crossed back into their country from abroad in 2025. For most, the fall of the government was the signal they had waited years for. But for others, the gates stayed shut — blocked by a different kind of politics.

In late January, 34 Australian women and children left Al Roj camp under Kurdish military escort, headed for Damascus and then home. About 50 km from the camp, Kurdish security forces received a call from officials in Damascus: the convoy would be “struck” if it tried to cross into government-controlled territory. They turned back.

Days later, 21-year-old Eva Dumani — an Albanian national who had spent 12 years in Syria, six of them detained in al-Hol — was reunited with her family in Tirana. She made it. The 34 Australians did not. “For years, governments claimed that difficulties negotiating with a non-state actor was why they couldn’t repatriate their citizens,” said Adam Coogle of Human Rights Watch. “With the fall of the SDF, that excuse no longer holds.”

The Return Wave

1.91 million came back. Return is not the same as recovery.

January 2025 brought 380,000 people home in a single month — the dam breaking. Every month after brought tens of thousands more. By December 2025, 1.91 million people had returned to their home communities inside Syria.

But the data cannot capture what waited at the end of those journeys. In July 2025, 64-year-old Iraqi Ibrahim Darwish finally made it home after four years in al-Hol. His relief lasted only until he understood the terms: to “regain his place in Iraqi society,” he was required to publicly disown his sons, accused of being ISIS fighters. His house was gone. His community “unrecognisable.”

“The communities expected to absorb these returnees are not ready,” said Sheikmous Ahmed. The families arriving were not the only ones carrying years of unresolved grief. So were the places they were returning to.

December 2025

The job the international community refused to plan for

3 : 1

The blue and amber dots on this map are not in different parts of the country. They are neighbors. Aleppo received more returnees than any other governorate and holds 1.28 million IDPs. Idleb saw 504,000 people return and still hosts nearly a million displaced. In Syria in December 2025, return and displacement are simultaneous conditions — in the same places, sometimes the same families.

For every person who came home, three remain displaced. The 1.91 million who returned represent the largest voluntary return Syria has seen since the war began. They are outnumbered three-to-one by those still living somewhere they did not choose.

Muzar Al Salloul, the SSU’s executive director, put the stakes plainly: “The children and women are not guilty of anything, but if communities continue to reject them, they will become vulnerable to recruitment by ISIS.”

For years, Western governments cited complexity as justification for inaction. Now that the camps are emptying, the work of reintegration falls to Syrian civil society — doing the job the international community refused to plan for, with a fraction of the resources it requires.

Methodology

Displacement and return figures come from UNHCR/OCHA’s Displacement Tracking Matrix for Syria, December 2025 — 8,325 community-level records covering all 14 governorates. IDP counts reflect populations living outside their area of origin within Syria; returnee counts track movements since December 8, 2024. Monthly return figures are drawn from the same DTM reporting series. US aid figures are from USAID and the UN Financial Tracking Service. Quotes from Abu Qaseem, Muzar Al Salloul, Sheikmous Ahmed, and Adam Coogle are drawn from the article “Leaving Limbo” (2026). The al-Hol population figure and foreign national estimates reflect U.S. intelligence assessments cited in contemporaneous reporting.

The map is built with Leaflet 1.9.4 on a CARTO dark tile layer. Each dot represents one monitored community; size and color encode population scale using a five-tier threshold system applied separately to IDP and returnee counts. All 8,325 style values are pre-computed at load time into typed arrays and interpolated across 550 ms transitions during scroll — an approach that keeps the animation loop free of per-frame computation. Scroll choreography uses two IntersectionObserver instances with no scroll event listeners. The monthly return chart is rendered with D3 v7. Video footage is from Al Jazeera and WION; sections play and pause natively via a third observer as they enter and exit the viewport.
